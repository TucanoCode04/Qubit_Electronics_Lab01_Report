The circuit shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ltspice} models a Josephson junction using a parallel RLC configuration.
The left branch, composed of the controlled source $B3$ and the capacitor $C1$, has the role of generating the model of the phase evolution across the junction. 
The source $B2$ models the Josephson supercurrent $I_s = I_c \sin(\phi)$, while $R1$ represents the normal resistive channel of the junction. 
Finally, $B1$ provides a time-dependent bias current that drives the junction.

The voltage across the junction, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:three_peaks_AC}, exhibits a series of peaks that correspond to the AC Josephson effect.
As the bias current exceeds the critical current $I_c$, the junction transitions into a voltage state where the phase evolves in time, leading to oscillations in the voltage.
The time intervals between the peaks decrease as the bias current increases, which is consistent with the Joseph voltage–frequency relation, where the frequency of oscillation is proportional to the voltage across the junction.

From the peak times reported in Table~\ref{peaks_times}, a clear trend is observed: the time intervals $\Delta t$ between consecutive voltage peaks decrease progressively. This indicates that the oscillation frequency of the junction is increasing over time. Such behavior is a direct manifestation of the AC Josephson effect.

When the bias current $I$ is increased beyond the critical threshold ($I_c = 10\,\mu\mathrm{A}$), the junction can no longer maintain a purely superconducting state with zero voltage. Instead, it enters the "voltage state," where the superconducting phase difference $\phi$ across the junction begins to evolve dynamically. According to the second Josephson relation:
\[
V(t) = \frac{\hbar}{2e} \frac{d\phi}{dt}
\]
each peak in the voltage trace corresponds to a "phase-slip" event, specifically a rapid $2\pi$ rotation of the phase. 

Since the mean voltage $\langle V \rangle$ across the junction increases as the bias current rises, the Josephson voltage--frequency relation:
\[
f = \frac{2e}{h}\langle V \rangle = \frac{1}{\Delta t}
\]
dictates that the frequency of these phase-slip events must increase. Consequently, the peaks in the simulation appear progressively closer in time, and the decrease in $\Delta t$ provides a direct temporal measurement of the acceleration of the superconducting phase. This effect is also evident in the amplitude and trend of the voltage peaks observed in the simulation: the peaks follow an overall increasing pattern, reflecting the higher mean voltage level at larger bias currents.






From the peak times reported in Table~\ref{peaks_times}, we observe that the time interval between consecutive peaks decreases slightly, meaning that the peaks become progressively closer in time. 
This behavior is expected because once the bias current exceeds the critical current ($I_c = 10\,\mu\mathrm{A}$), the junction enters the voltage state and the phase evolves according to the Josephson voltage–frequency relation 
\[
f = \frac{2e}{h}V .
\]
As the bias current increases, the mean value of voltage across the junction also increases due to the resistive branch of the model, and this can be observed in the ascending pattern of the voltage peaks.
